What this atlas is

A data-visualization and decision-support prototype that connects spatial layers, emissions hotspots, regional implementation constraints and evidence-based transition pathways.

What this atlas is not

It is not a parcel-level rewetting suitability map, not a substitute for hydrological planning, and not a general educational Mooratlas replica.

01 · Extent

Peatlands are not everywhere — they are spatially concentrated.

The first question is geographic: where are peat-dominated areas and peat-in-soil-mosaic areas located? The map starts with peatland extent before adding emissions pressure.

02 · Absolute pressure

Total emissions identify national climate relevance.

Absolute drained-organic-soil emissions show which countries dominate the global mitigation signal. This is the first prioritisation view, but not the only one.

03 · Intensity

Emission density changes the interpretation.

Density asks a different question: where is pressure concentrated relative to the mapped drained organic-soil area? The same geography now tells a different story.

04 · Read together

Transition priorities need both views.

High totals indicate global climate relevance. High density indicates intensity. A transition atlas should not force users to choose one metric; it should show why both are needed.

05 · Europe frame

Europe needs its own regional map frame.

The story now changes scale. Country borders make the European frame readable before the peatland pattern is added back in.

06 · European peat context

European peatlands are spatially concentrated, not evenly spread.

The European frame shows peatland context with higher regional clarity. This sets up the next move: from continental structure to the national implementation layer in Germany.

\n
07 · Germany frame

Germany is where peatland transition becomes an implementation problem.

The story now shifts from continental patterns to national planning geography. In Germany, federal structures, land use, drainage history, and soil classification determine where transition is technically and politically feasible.

08 · Thuenen Kulisse

The Thuenen Kulisse identifies the national peat and organic-soils target area.

The one-colour layer first establishes the spatial footprint: where peat and organic soils are relevant for mitigation, rewetting, agricultural adaptation, and land-use planning.

09 · Moor and soil types

The type distinction is not cosmetic — it changes the transition logic.

Fen, raised bog, transformed peat soils, deeply ploughed classes, and covered organic soils imply different hydrological constraints, restoration potential, agricultural trade-offs, and evidence needs.

10 · Baden-Württemberg frame

Baden-Württemberg narrows the national frame to a regional planning scale.

The story moves from the national Thuenen Kulisse to a federal-state frame. This scale is closer to implementation, but still needs regional soil evidence before transition pathways can be interpreted locally.

11 · BK50 peat and wetland soils

BK50 shows the regional peat and wetland soil context.

This first BW layer deliberately shows all mapped BK50 peat and wetland soil features as one extent. It does not yet classify agricultural use or rewetting suitability; that requires a later combination with land-use evidence.

Method and uncertainty

How to read this prototype

This Atlas is a literature-driven prototype. It is designed to make evidence, assumptions and transition hypotheses visible, not to replace local hydrological-economic modelling.

Qualitative scores

Scores are literature-informed expert codings, not statistical estimates. The scale is deliberately simple: 1 = low, 2 = medium, 3 = high.

Evidence-map points

Map points are approximate visual anchors for study regions, national model studies, review clusters and policy nodes. They are not exact field-site coordinates.

South Germany fit

South Germany fit is a transfer hypothesis based on international literature. It is not a validated regional model result and must be checked against local hydrology, farm structure, machinery access and markets.

Publication boundary

The public repository should contain code, derived metadata and own coding tables. It should not contain copyrighted PDFs, confidential project data, licence-unclear raw rasters or unpublished partner data.
